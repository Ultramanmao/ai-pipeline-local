# Z-Image Turbo on 8 GB: Is 4 Steps Enough? A 4-Step vs 20-Step Comparison

> Date: 2026-07-20 · GPU: RTX 5060 Laptop 8GB (sm_120) · CUDA: cu132 · Framework: ComfyUI + ZImagePowerNodes · OS: Windows 11 native
> Release: https://github.com/Ultramanmao/ai-pipeline-local/releases/tag/v13.0

## TL;DR

Z-Image Turbo is marketed as a 4-step turbo model. But is 4 steps really enough? Or should you run 20 steps for better quality?

I ran a controlled comparison: same seed, same prompt, same resolution — only steps varied (4 vs 20). The answer:

| Metric | 4 steps | 20 steps | Delta |
|--------|---------|----------|-------|
| Time | **35s** | 74s | **2.1× slower** |
| Sharpness (Laplacian var) | 82.6 | 89.6 | **+8.5%** |
| Brightness | 67.7 | 66.2 | −2.2% |
| Color std (R/G/B) | 56/47/40 | 56/47/39 | Negligible |
| Image entropy | 7.24 | 7.17 | −1% |
| Pixels changed >10 | — | 23.7% | Minor |
| Pixels changed >30 | — | **5.1%** | Negligible |

**4 steps is enough.** 20 steps gives +8.5% more sharpness at 2.1× the cost. On a Turbo model, the extra steps are almost entirely diminishing returns.

## Speed: 2× for 5× More Steps

| Steps | Total time | Step time (avg) | Speedup |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|---------|
| 4 | 35s | 8.75s/step | Baseline |
| 20 | 74s | 3.7s/step | 2.1× slower total |

The 4-step run is dominated by model loading and text encoder overhead (~17s on first run). The actual step time for 4 steps is ~18s. For 20 steps, the per-step cost drops to 3.7s because the model is already loaded.

**Bottom line:** 4 steps is 35s, 20 steps is 74s. On a cold start, the gap is only 2.1×. On a warm ComfyUI (model already cached), the gap would be larger — roughly 5×.

## Quality: Visually Indistinguishable

I ran objective metrics on both outputs:

### Sharpness
Laplacian variance: 82.6 (4 steps) → 89.6 (20 steps). That's +8.5%. The difference is visible only under pixel-level zoom. At normal viewing, both images look equally sharp.

### Brightness and Color
Virtually identical. Mean pixel brightness shifted from 67.7 to 66.2 (−2.2%). Color standard deviation is nearly the same across all three RGB channels. The 20-step version is slightly darker and slightly less saturated — but by a margin that wouldn't survive a double-blind test.

### Image Entropy
7.24 vs 7.17. Essentially identical. The "information content" of both images is the same — the 20-step version doesn't add meaningful detail.

### Pixel-Level Differences
Only 23.7% of pixels differ by more than 10 (out of 255), and only 5.1% differ by more than 30. The two images are 95% identical at the pixel level.

## 4-Step Is the Sweet Spot

Z-Image Turbo is designed as a turbo model. The "turbo" in its name means it was trained with a progressive distillation process that converges in 4 steps. Running 20 steps on a turbo model is like trying to bake a microwave meal for 20 minutes — you get more heat, not more cooking.

| Use case | Steps | Why |
|----------|-------|-----|
| **Iteration / prototyping** | 4 | 35s/image, good enough for prompt testing |
| **Final output** | 4 | 95% of the quality at half the time |
| **Fine details / text** | 8–10 | Rare cases where every pixel counts |
| **20 steps** | ❌ | Diminishing returns — 2× time for 8% more sharpness |

---

## The Rule: Turbo Models Don't Scale with Steps

This isn't just Z-Image. It's a property of distilled turbo models:

> **Turbo models converge in their training step count. Adding steps beyond that gives diminishing returns.**

- SDXL Turbo: 4 steps sweet spot
- Z-Image Turbo: 4 steps sweet spot
- Flux.2 Klein: 8 steps sweet spot (non-turbo architecture)
- Flux.1-dev: 30 steps for full quality (non-turbo GGUF)

The only scenario where 20 steps helps is if you're using an **old_scheduler** or **noise_injection** mode — both of which are off by default. With the default turbo scheduler, the model converges at step 4.
